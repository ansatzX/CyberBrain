# Reading Papers

Based on three writing guides, presented independently.

## Source A: Mack, *How to Write a Good Scientific Paper* (SPIE Press, 2018)

Chapters read: Ch1 (Getting Started), Ch2 (Structure and Organization), Ch3 (Language and Style), Ch6 (Abstract and Title), Ch7 (What an Editor Looks For).

### A.1 The IMRAD Structure (Ch. 2)

78% of papers in Mack's journal sample used some variation of IMRAD. Mack also gives an alternative framing: CEC (claim, evidence, commentary).

**Introduction.** Mack cites Swales' three-move model: (1) establish a territory; (2) establish a niche — indicate a gap, raise a question, or challenge prior work; (3) occupy that niche. Common pitfalls: unnecessary background, exaggerating importance, failing to make clear what research questions the paper addresses.

**Method.** Two goals: enable reproducibility AND enable the reader to judge validity. Internal validity = conclusions supported by results. External validity = generalizability. "Rarely does a method section explain why one approach was chosen over another. Nobody reproduces other people's work anymore, or so the thought goes. I find this attitude mistaken and often self-serving." Common pitfalls: including results in Methods, extraneous details, chronological rather than logical order. "If the raw results are not going to be presented, then a description of the data-reduction procedures is required."

**Results and Discussion.** Results "should be very short. It is simply a presentation of the results obtained." Must "clearly designate those results that are new (never before published)." Mack's critical warning: **"Evidence does not explain itself."** Discussion inverts the Introduction (specific → general). Stages: summarize results, discuss whether expected/unexpected, compare to previous work, interpret/explain, hypothesize about generality. "Any problems or shortcomings encountered during the course of the work should also be discussed." Common pitfalls: ignoring contradictory results, "drawing conclusions from results without sound logical arguments to back them up."

**Conclusions.** "Should be more than a summary." Provide "the most general claims that can be supported by the evidence." Speculation acceptable only if "clearly labeled as speculative." Common pitfalls: repeating the abstract, introducing new evidence.

### A.2 The Scientific Style (Ch. 3)

Mack defines scientific style through five stands (after Thomas & Turner):

| Stand | Definition |
|---|---|
| Truth | Objective reality exists independent of writer/reader. Discoverable, verifiable. Writer claims no privileged access. |
| Presentation | Values ranked: accuracy > precision > clarity > concision > grace. "Accuracy means that all new knowledge claims are justified and verifiable." Two ways to claim too much: claims in opposition to facts, and claims unsupported by facts. "Any claim that cannot be verified given the information provided in the paper is as good as unsupported." |
| Scene | A symposium talk — audience interested, will save questions for the end. Formal, professional tone. |
| Cast | Peers: intelligent, willing to put in effort, but only if worth their while. "Sometimes experts in the specific niche...but usually not." |
| Thought and Language | Language (including mathematics) is fully capable. "Sloppy use of words is considered a sign of sloppy thinking." |

On the passive voice: "the scientific stance on truth makes the scientist replaceable; anybody could have done the same experiments." First-person pronouns are appropriate when authors are proposing or suggesting — acts where they are not replaceable.

### A.3 Abstract and Title (Ch. 6)

Mack frames the abstract and title as **matchmaking** — getting the right paper to the right reader. He advocates the structured abstract with five components: Background, Aim, Approach, Results, Conclusions. Write it as a structured abstract, then delete subheadings and combine.

Mack cites a 1999 study of six major medical journals: about **40% of abstracts contained information inconsistent with the body**. His three-part abstract test: (1) Is all abstract information consistent with the body? (2) Can all abstract information be found in the body? (3) Is the important information in the abstract?

### A.4 Editor Criteria (Ch. 7)

Four criteria: **Scope** (does content match journal scope?), **Quality** (of work and of writing — "if the initial quality of the writing is not sufficiently high, it may be nearly impossible to judge the quality of the work itself"), **Novelty** (at least 50% of results should be new; "an effective literature search and comprehensive citations are a requirement to establish what is novel"), **Significance** (problem importance × advance size).

Mack also discusses the **null result bias**: "In science, the only failed experiment is one that does not lead to a conclusion." Author-submission bias accounts for most of the publication bias — negative results never get submitted.

### A.5 Paper Types Covered by Mack

Mack is focused on the standard research paper. He does not provide separate chapters on reviews, methods papers, benchmark papers, grant proposals, or theses. His Ch. 2 notes that "For some research articles, it is the method that is novel. For this case, a much more detailed description is required." For theory papers: "a common variation of the IMRAD structure separates the theory...into its own preceding section."

## Source B: Gastel & Day, *How to Write and Publish a Scientific Paper* (8th ed., 2016)

Chapters read: Ch4 (Definition), Ch7 (Title), Ch9 (Abstract), Ch10 (Introduction), Ch11 (Methods), Ch12 (Results), Ch13 (Discussion), Ch23 (Review), Ch35 (Thesis), Ch37 (Grant Proposals).

### B.1 Definition of a Scientific Paper (Ch. 4)

CBE/CSE definition: an acceptable primary scientific publication must be the **first disclosure** containing **sufficient information** for peers to (1) assess observations, (2) repeat experiments, and (3) evaluate intellectual processes. It must be "susceptible to sensory perception, essentially permanent, available to the scientific community without restriction, and available for regular screening by one or more of the major recognized secondary services." Conference reports, theses, and government literature do not normally qualify. "If a scientific paper meets all the other tests, it is not validly published if it is published in the wrong place."

The IMRAD logic: What question was studied? (Introduction) How was it studied? (Methods) What were the findings? (Results) What do these findings mean? (Discussion).

### B.2 Title (Ch. 7)

"A good title is the fewest possible words that adequately describe the contents of the paper." The title is a label, not a sentence. Faulty syntax is "the most common error in defective titles, and certainly the most damaging one in terms of comprehension." The title will be read by thousands; the paper by few. Waste words: "Studies on," "Investigations on," "Observations on," and opening articles (A, An, The). Rosner's "assertive sentence title" (AST) — a full sentence with a verb — should generally be avoided because it "boldly states a conclusion that is then stated more tentatively in the summary." On indexing: "An improperly titled paper may be virtually lost and never reach its intended audience."

### B.3 Abstract (Ch. 9)

"An abstract should be viewed as a miniature version of the paper." It should provide a brief summary of each main section: introduction, materials and methods, results, and discussion. Four components: (1) state the principal objectives and scope, (2) describe the methods employed, (3) summarize the results, (4) state the principal conclusions. Most or all should be in the past tense. The abstract should never give any information or conclusion not stated in the paper. Literature must not be cited (except in rare instances). Normally should not refer to tables and figures.

Two types distinguished:
- **Informative abstract**: condenses the paper — states the problem, method, principal data, and conclusions. Can sometimes supplant the need for reading the full paper. Used as "heading" abstracts in research papers. "However, before citing a paper, you should read it in its entirety because some abstracts — surely not yours, though! — do not convey an entirely accurate picture of the research."
- **Indicative (descriptive) abstract**: indicates subjects dealt with, much like a table of contents. Should NOT be used as heading abstracts in research papers, but may be used in review papers, conference reports, and government reports.

McGirr (1973): "When writing the abstract, remember that it will be published by itself, and should be self-contained. That is, it should contain no bibliographic, figure, or table references." Write the paper before the abstract. Word economy is paramount: "E=mc²" as the shortest abstract ever written. "If you cannot make a good impression in your abstract, your cause may be lost. Very often, the reviewer may be perilously close to a final judgment of your manuscript after reading the abstract alone." "Usually, a good abstract is followed by a good paper; a poor abstract is a harbinger of woes to come."

On suspense: "Reading a scientific article isn't the same as reading a detective story. We want to know from the start that the butler did it" (Ratnoff 1981).

### B.4 Introduction (Ch. 10)

The introduction should supply sufficient background for readers to understand and evaluate the results without needing to consult prior publications. It should present the problem and the solution. A good introduction follows the **funnel format**: general → specific.

Gastel & Day provide rules: (1) present the nature and scope of the problem, (2) review pertinent literature, (3) state the method of investigation (and reasons for choosing it), (4) state the principal results, (5) state the principal conclusions. "The introduction provides a road map from problem to solution. This map is so important that a bit of redundancy with the abstract is often desirable."

Exceptions: journals in some disciplines favor extensive literature reviews, almost resembling a review article within the paper. Some journals make this a separate section after the introduction. Conventions in the field and target journal requirements take precedence. Define specialized terms and abbreviations.

### B.5 Materials and Methods (Ch. 11)

"Enough information must be given so that the experiments could be reproduced by a competent colleague." A good test: give a copy of the finished manuscript to a colleague and ask if he or she can follow the methodology.

Key guidance:
- If the method is new (unpublished), provide all needed detail.
- If published in a journal, give the literature reference. For methods well known to readers, only the reference is needed. For methods readers might not be familiar with, add a brief description.
- "If several alternative methods are commonly employed, it is useful to identify your method briefly as well as to cite the reference."
- Statistical analyses: "your paper should emphasize the data, not the statistics." Ordinary methods should be used without comment; advanced or unusual methods may require a literature citation.
- Include strain tables for microbial strains/mutants, property tables for chemical compounds.
- Do not include results in this section. "There is only one rule for a properly written materials and methods section: Enough information must be given so that the experiments could be reproduced by a competent colleague."
- On passive voice: in the methods section, passive voice often can validly be used because "although what was done must be specified, who did it is often irrelevant."
- Most common error: "to state the action without, when necessary, stating the agent of the action" — e.g., "To determine its respiratory quotient, the organism was..." (the organism cannot make that determination).

### B.6 Results (Ch. 12)

The Results section is the most important part — it constitutes the new knowledge contributed. "The whole paper must stand or fall on the basis of the results. Thus, the results must be presented with crystal clarity." Einstein quoted: "If you are out to describe the truth, leave elegance to the tailor."

Key guidance:
- "The fool collects facts; the wise man selects them" (John Wesley Powell). Present representative data, not all data.
- Avoid redundancy: the most common fault is repeating in words what is already apparent from figures and tables. Even worse: presenting all or many data from tables in the text.
- When citing figures and tables, be concise. Do not say "It is clearly shown in Table 1 that nocillin inhibited the growth." Say "Nocillin inhibited the growth (Table 1)."
- Define negative aspects: "it is often good insurance to state what you did not find under the conditions of your experiments."
- If statistics are used, they should be meaningful statistics.
- On supplementary material online: increasingly, journals post additional data, tables, figures online. Consult the journal's instructions.

### B.7 Discussion (Ch. 13)

"The discussion is harder to define than the other sections. Thus, it is usually the hardest section to write. And, whether you know it or not, many papers are rejected by journal editors because of a faulty discussion, even though the data of the paper might be both valid and interesting."

Key guidance:
- Many discussion sections are too long and verbose. Doug Savile's "squid technique": "the author is doubtful about his facts or his reasoning and retreats behind a protective cloud of ink."
- Avoid wordier constructions: say "I found that..." or "We conclude that..." rather than "It was found in the present investigation that..."
- State what the results mean. Do not recapitulate the results. The discussion explains; the results present.
- "Try to present the principles, relationships, and generalizations shown by the Results. And bear in mind, in a good Discussion, you discuss — you do not recapitulate — the Results."
- Point out any exceptions or lack of correlation, and define unsettled points. Show how your results and interpretations agree or contrast with previously published work.
- Discuss theoretical implications and possible practical applications. State your conclusions as clearly as possible. Summarize evidence for each conclusion.

### B.8 Review Paper (Ch. 23)

"A review paper is not an original publication in the usual sense, though it can be valuable scholarship." Typically 10–50 published pages. "The really good review papers are much more than annotated bibliographies. They offer critical evaluation of the published literature and often provide important conclusions based on that literature."

Organization differs from IMRAD: "Greatly expand the introduction; delete the materials and methods (unless original data are being presented or you will say how you identified and chose the literature to include); delete the results; and expand the discussion." A cardinal rule: prepare an outline. "If your review is organized properly, the overall scope of the review will be well defined."

Types of reviews:
- **Critical evaluation reviews**: old-line review journals "prefer, and some demand, authoritative and critical evaluations."
- **Compilation/annotation reviews**: "Annual Review of" series — "designed to compile and to annotate but not necessarily to evaluate."
- **Historical reviews**: organized chronologically; "there is still a place for history."
- **State-of-the-art reviews**: "provide a new understanding of a rapidly moving field."
- **Systematic reviews**: address "a clearly formulated question" and use "systematic and explicit methods to identify, select, and critically appraise relevant research." Include a methods section (databases searched, search terms, dates/languages, inclusion/exclusion criteria). Cite PRISMA statement.

Audience: read by peers, people in related fields, and students. "Your style of writing should be much less technical than for a research paper. Jargon and specialized abbreviations must be eliminated or carefully explained."

### B.9 Thesis / Dissertation (Ch. 35)

Purpose: "to prove that the candidate can do and communicate such research." Unlike a scientific paper, a thesis may address more than one topic. "The concept that a thesis must be a bulky 200-page tome is wrong, dead wrong. Many 200-page theses contain only 50 pages of good science."

European vs. U.S. theses: European theses are "designed to show that the candidate has reached maturity and can both do science and write science. Such theses may be submitted after some years of work and a number of primary publications, with the thesis itself being a 'review paper' that brings it all together."

Structure: several chapters, sometimes IMRAD-based, sometimes a chapter per study. Common components: title page, acknowledgments, abstract, table of contents, list of figures/tables, list of abbreviations, appendixes. "The introduction or a separate literature-review chapter generally should present a thorough review of previous work...it is often desirable to go back into the history of your subject."

Timing: "Allow at least 6 months." Begin writing long before due. Not written from beginning to end — start literature review early, draft methods soon after used, often draft introduction last. "A good thesis is likely to require multiple drafts."

Thesis is not itself publishable: "Extracting one or more publications from a thesis generally entails considerable trimming and condensation."

### B.10 Grant Proposal (Ch. 37)

"A good proposal generally includes background information relating to the proposed endeavor, a statement of goals, a research plan, a budget, and information about the qualifications of those who are to do the work." If lengthy: title page, abstract, letter of transmittal, table of contents, description of predicted impact, dissemination plan, facilities information, references.

Preliminary proposals/letters of intent: can save work and help shape research. Require "much of the same rigorous thinking" as a full proposal. Common parts: background, goals, research/program plan, budget, CVs.

Key guidance:
- "Only if a proposal matches the priorities of a funding source is it likely to be funded." Consult staff (program officers). Look at examples of successful proposals. "In preparing grant proposals, as in other scientific writing, following good models saves time, avoids guesswork, and promotes success."
- Start long before deadline — at least 6 months for a lengthy proposal.
- "Read all instructions carefully, and follow them precisely." Proposals not complying may be disqualified without review.
- "Match the technical level of the proposal to the background of the reviewers." Government agencies: fairly technical. Private sources with lay boards: no more technical than a popular science article.
- All key information should appear in the main body; reviewers typically are not obligated to read appendixes.

## Source C: Whitesides, "Whitesides' Group: Writing a Paper" (*Advanced Materials*, 2004, 16, No. 15, August 4)

Full text read via image analysis of all three rendered pages.

### C.1 What Is a Scientific Paper?

"A paper is an organized description of hypotheses, data and conclusions, intended to instruct the reader. Papers are a central part of research. If your research does not generate papers, it might just as well not have been done. 'Interesting and unpublished' is equivalent to 'non-existent'."

"Realize that your objective in research is to formulate and test hypotheses, to draw conclusions from these tests, and to teach these conclusions to others. Your objective is not to 'collect data'."

A paper is also "a structure for planning your research in progress. If you clearly understand the purpose and form of a paper, it can be immensely useful to you in organizing and conducting your research. A good outline for the paper is also a good plan for the research program."

### C.2 The Outline Method

"I emphasize the central place of an outline in writing papers, preparing seminars, and planning research. I especially believe that for you, and for me, it is most efficient to write papers from outlines."

**Definition**: "a written plan of the organization of a paper, including the data on which it rests. You should, in fact, think of an outline as a carefully organized and presented set of data, with attendant objectives, hypotheses, and conclusions, rather than an outline of text."

**Why outlines work**: "An outline itself contains little text. If you and I can agree on the details of the outline (that is, on the data and organization), the supporting text can be assembled fairly easily. If we do not agree on the outline, any text is useless. Much of the time in writing a paper goes into the text; most of the thought goes into the organization of the data and into the analysis."

**Iteration**: "It can be relatively efficient in time to go through several (even many) cycles of an outline before beginning to write text; writing many versions of the full text of a paper is slow."

### C.3 How to Construct an Outline

Start with a blank piece of paper. Write down all important ideas in any order. Ask: "Why did I do this work?"; "What does it mean?"; "What hypotheses did I mean to test?"; "What ones did I actually test?"; "What were the results?"; "What measurements did I make?"; "What compounds? How were they characterized?"; "Sketch possible equations, figures, and schemes."

Then organize into three major heaps: (1) Introduction, (2) Results and Discussion, (3) Conclusions. Within each, "concentrate on organizing the data. Construct figures, tables, and schemes to present the data as clearly and compactly as possible. This process can be slow — I may sketch a figure five to ten times in different ways trying to decide how it is most clear."

**Timing**: "Do not, under any circumstances, wait until the collection of data is 'complete' before starting to write an outline. No project is ever complete."

**Iteration with the advisor**: "It usually takes four to five iterations (often with additional experiments) to agree on an outline."

### C.4 What an Outline Should Contain

1. **Title**
2. **Authors**
3. **Abstract** — "Do not write an abstract. That can be done when the paper is complete."
4. **Introduction** — "The first paragraph or two should be written out completely. Pay particular attention to the opening sentence. Ideally, it should state concisely the objective of the work, and indicate why this objective is important." Elements: objectives; justification; background (who else has done what); guidance to the reader (what to watch for); summary.
5. **Results and Discussion** — Usually combined. Organized by major topics with boldface subheadings. Section headings should be "as specific and information-rich as possible," e.g., "The Rate of Self-Exchange Decreases with the Polarity of the Solvent" rather than "Measurement of Rates."
6. **Conclusions** — "Summarize the conclusions as a list of short phrases or sentences. Do not repeat what is in the Results section. The Conclusions section should be just that, and not a summary. It should add a new, higher level of analysis, and should indicate explicitly the significance of the work."
7. **Experimental** — "Include, in the correct order to correspond to the order in the Results section, all of the paragraph subheadings."

### C.5 Summary Principles

- Start outlines early. "Do not wait until the 'end'. The end may never come."
- "Organize the outline and the paper around easily assimilated data — tables, equations, figures, schemes — rather than around text."
- "Organize in order of importance, not in chronological order." Neophytes give "a recitation of their experimental program, starting with their cherished initial failures and leading up to a climactic successful finale. This approach is completely wrong. Start with the most important results."
- "Shorter papers are easier to read than longer ones."

### C.6 Points of Style

- Do not use nouns as adjectives: "ATP formation" → "formation of ATP"
- "This" must always be followed by a noun: "This reaction is fast" not "This is a fast reaction"
- Experimental results in past tense: "Addition of water gave product" not "gives"
- Active voice: "The solution turned red" not "It was observed that the solution turned red"
- Complete all comparisons: "higher using bromine than chlorine" not "higher using bromine"
- Double-spaced; two spaces after colons and periods; generous margins.
- Follow ACS style. Sources: the journals, previous group papers, *The ACS Style Guide*.

### C.7 Coverage

Whitesides covers only the standard research paper. His contribution is the outline method — a specific workflow for organizing a paper around data rather than text — and the principle that a paper's outline is also the research plan.
